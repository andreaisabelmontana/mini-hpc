miniHPC

A whole supercomputing centre, shrunk to an app you can poke at. miniHPC lets you submit jobs, hand them resource requests, and watch a scheduler place them across a pool of workers in real time — queues, priorities, contention and all. I rebuilt it from scratch because the fastest way to understand a cluster is to build a tiny one you can see thinking.

What it is

A cluster you can watch think

Real HPC schedulers like Slurm are powerful and almost entirely invisible — you submit a job, wait, and get results, with the interesting decisions hidden inside a black box. miniHPC turns that box transparent. It models the core loop of any batch system: jobs arrive with resource demands, a scheduler decides what runs where and when, workers execute, and the queue drains.

Because it's small and visible, you can feel the trade-offs that make scheduling hard. Pack jobs tightly and big ones starve; favour the big ones and throughput drops. Watching the queue rearrange under different policies teaches more than any diagram of one ever could.

Architecture

How a job moves through

Every tick of the simulated cluster runs the same scheduling cycle:

  1. Submit

    A job enters the queue with its resource request and expected runtime.

  2. Schedule

    The scheduler picks the next job that fits an available worker.

  3. Allocate

    Reserve the worker's cores and memory for that job.

  4. Run

    Execute it for its duration, then release the resources.

  5. Repeat

    Re-evaluate the queue and keep the cluster as busy as the policy allows.

Reflection

What rebuilding it taught me

  • Scheduling is a balancing act. Throughput, fairness and turnaround pull against each other — no policy wins on all three.
  • Big jobs starve. Greedy packing keeps utilisation high but can leave large jobs waiting forever — which is why backfill exists.
  • Resources are the real constraint. A job isn't schedulable just because a worker is idle; it has to fit.
  • Visibility is the teacher. Seeing the queue rearrange under a policy change makes the abstraction click instantly.
  • Small models explain big systems. A toy cluster captures the exact tensions a real one faces — minus the YAML.
